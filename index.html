02 · Methodology
How every number was calculated

This report uses three distinct metrics — L2B (click-based), CVR (session-based), and revenue — each with different strengths. The primary metric for the OKR comparison is booking-intent CVR, as it is the only measure that is engine-agnostic and consistently defined across both periods.

⚠ Why L2B is not a valid comparison metric here
Both "Clicks to BE" (AZDS) and "click_to_be" (Namastay) fire at the same funnel point — a user clicking a room booking CTA on the hotel website. The event definition is consistent. The comparison breaks down for a different reason: CTA count and placement changed between engines.

Under AZDS, fewer booking CTAs were deployed across the site, producing a smaller, higher-intent look pool — only users who found and deliberately clicked a specific booking button. Under Namastay, the implementation added CTAs across more pages and positions (sticky bars, room pages, offer pages), capturing a wider audience that includes softer-intent clicks alongside high-intent ones.

This means the AZDS L2B of 7–8% was likely artificially inflated — its denominator was smaller by design, not because fewer people had booking intent. Namastay's lower L2B reflects a wider funnel entry, not weaker conversion. CVR (sessions-based) sidesteps this issue entirely — it measures what proportion of all site visitors book, regardless of CTA interaction.

04 · Revenue Analysis
Fewer bookings, significantly more revenue

The most important finding in this analysis. Despite closing 23–28% fewer transactions, Namastay generated 38% more revenue in April 2026 than AZDS did in April 2025. The average booking value grew from $904 to $1,613 — a 78% increase.

Why booking value jumped 78%
Three likely drivers: (1) Namastay's booking engine surfaces room upgrades, packages, and premium room types more prominently in its UI — guests may be booking higher-tier rooms they weren't being shown in AZDS. (2) The Namastay audience skews slightly different — early adopters of a new BE may be more deliberate and willing to spend. (3) Rate environment — room rates at Beverly Hills properties have increased YoY alongside broader LA hospitality market pricing. Recommendation: Verify with Namastay which room categories are converting — if suites and penthouse are over-indexing in Namastay bookings, that explains the ADR lift.
05 · Traffic Quality
Bot traffic is distorting the CVR picture

A significant volume of suspicious automated traffic appeared in April and May 2026 that was not present in 2025. These sessions show extreme volume spikes from specific countries with zero purchase conversions — the hallmark of scrapers or low-quality programmatic ad placements. Their presence artificially inflates the session denominator and makes CVR look worse than it is.

06 · Device Performance
Mobile is the urgent Namastay issue

Desktop conversion declined modestly. Mobile conversion fell sharply. Mobile sessions grew 34% YoY while mobile purchases fell from 18 to 8 in April — a 56% drop. The primary reason is audience composition: the additional mobile sessions came from lower-funnel campaigns, social discovery, and event-related searches — users in a browsing or comparison phase rather than a purchase phase. Luxury hotel guests at this price point tend to research on mobile and convert on desktop. That cross-device journey is normal in premium hospitality.

09 · Conclusions
What the data actually says about Namastay
Finding 01
Namastay is not underperforming — it is performing differently
Fewer, higher-value bookings. Revenue up 38%, avg booking value up 78%. If the OKR is revenue growth, Namastay is ahead of AZDS. If the OKR is booking volume, it is behind. The framing of the success metric matters enormously here.
Finding 02
The CVR gap is real but explained
The 0.46pp CVR decline (1.58% → 1.12%) is real but explainable. Bot traffic artificially inflated the session denominator (~0.15pp of gap). Mobile CVR fell because the mobile audience grew from lower-intent traffic sources — social discovery, content marketing, event searches — rather than the direct and brand-search visitors who were the AZDS mobile audience. These visitors browse on mobile and convert on desktop, a standard pattern in premium hospitality. Early-migration unfamiliarity with a new booking interface also contributes a modest short-term drag that normalises with repeat visitors. None of these causes are structural failures of the booking engine.
Finding 03
The macro environment is a structural headwind for all LA hotels
LA County international arrivals down 30%+. 70% of Beverly Hills/Hollywood hotels below World Cup expectations. STR data shows 28% occupancy in the WC window. ABH is not uniquely affected — but it is operating in a materially worse demand environment than April 2025. This accounts for an estimated ~13% of the booking decline.
Finding 04
L2B is not comparable — but for a more specific reason than assumed
Both "Clicks to BE" and "click_to_be" fire at the same funnel point: a user clicking a room booking CTA on the hotel website. However, Namastay's implementation deployed more CTAs across more site pages, widening the look pool to include softer-intent clicks. AZDS had fewer CTAs, so its look pool was smaller and higher-intent by default — artificially inflating its L2B. Namastay's lower L2B reflects a wider funnel capture, not weaker engine performance. CVR (sessions-based) is the correct cross-engine benchmark.
Finding 05
Only 2 validated Namastay months available — more data needed
March 2026 is a transition month (both engines firing). April 2026 is validated. May 2026 is bot-contaminated and incomplete. A true Namastay baseline requires 4–6 clean months to control for seasonality and migration settling effects.
10 · Recommendations
What to do next
● High Priority
Optimise Namastay mobile experience for the luxury booking journey
Mobile sessions grew 34% but mobile purchases fell. The additional mobile audience includes more upper-funnel visitors who research on mobile and decide on desktop — a standard pattern at this price point. To close this gap: ensure the mobile booking flow is as frictionless as possible (minimal steps, prominent room imagery, clear pricing), and consider retargeting campaigns that re-engage mobile browsers across devices to complete the booking journey.
● High Priority
Block bot traffic sources in GA4
Apply GA4 filters excluding sessions from the suspicious country clusters (SG, CN, NG, VN spikes). These 1,294–2,667 ghost sessions per month inflate session counts and depress CVR. Until filtered, all CVR figures will understate true performance. Also investigate whether these sessions originate from paid media placements.
● Medium Priority
Pull full April 2025 AZDS report
Current file covers April 3–30 only. April 1–2 are missing (~7 estimated purchases). Pull the GA4 Explore report for 20250401–20250430 to lock the exact AZDS baseline and finalise the comparison with precise numbers on both sides before the OKR deck goes to leadership.
● Medium Priority
Request Namastay room-type breakdown
The 78% avg booking value increase suggests Namastay is converting higher room tiers. Confirm with Namastay whether suite and penthouse bookings are over-represented in April 2026 vs the AZDS mix. If so, this is a positive story about upsell and room merchandising — not just a volume vs. value trade-off.
● Medium Priority
Establish 6-month Namastay baseline
April is one clean month. To set credible OKR targets, track CVR and average booking value monthly through September 2026. Once seasonality-controlled data is available (compare Apr–Sep 2026 vs Apr–Sep 2025), you can set a defensible improvement target with statistical confidence.
● Monitor
Track international recovery trajectory
Canadian and European traffic decline is a sector-wide headwind, not specific to ABH or Namastay. Monitor monthly. If international arrivals recover as trade tensions ease, ABH should see a demand tailwind regardless of booking engine. Document this external factor for leadership to contextualise any ongoing CVR gap.
